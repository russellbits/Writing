# The Iron Minds Come Hither
### *Being a Truthful Account of Strange and Terrible New Creatures, Set Down in Great Haste by One Who Hath Witnessed Their Coming*

---

## A Prologue Wherein the Author Begs Thy Patience

Hear me, good citizens, and shut not thine ears, though what I tell thee shall seem the ravings of a man who hath supped too long upon bad wine and moonlit philosophy. I beg thy patience, as a man begs the surgeon not to put away his blade mid-surgery, for what I cut open before thee today is the very belly of our coming age.

Think thee back to the winter before the great Plague walked among us. Dost thou remember? The markets were fat as aldermen at feast. Thy children skipped to their schoolhouses. Thou didst press the flesh of strangers with no more thought than one gives to breathing. Then, in the span of a fortnight — scarcely longer than it takes for milk to turn — the whole world became a different country. Thy house became thy prison. Thy children became thy pupils. And thou didst hoard squares of rag as though they were gold doubloons.

I tell thee now: what approacheth is to the Plague as a great tempest is to a man sneezing.

I have spent six years among the scholars and artificers who fashion these new minds from numbers and lightning — for that is the closest I can describe it in honest tongue — and I write to thee not as a man of learning who hath solved the riddle, but as a sailor who hath smelled land before the fog hath cleared. I smell it now. And the land ahead is vast and strange and utterly unlike anything thy maps describe.

## Of How It Happened First to Me, and Why This Matters to Thee

For many seasons, these Iron Minds — these creatures wrought not of flesh and bone but of something more like... the crystallized thought of ten thousand libraries, animated by a force no alchemist hath yet named — were improving, but slowly. Like a student who grows measurably more clever with each passing year, yet remains always a student.

Then, in the early months of this present year, two great houses of artificers — one called *Openai* and another called *Anthropic* — released new minds into the world upon the very same day. And I confess to thee: when I saw what these minds could do, I felt the water rising about my chest. Not metaphorically, as poets use the device. I mean that the chill of it found my bones.

I am a builder by trade. I tell these Iron Minds what I wish to be built — in plain speech, as I tell thee now — and I walk away from my table. For four hours I am absent. I return. The thing is built. Not roughhewn. Not needing my hammer to finish it. *Done.* Done as a master craftsman would do it, who hath worked his trade for twenty years, and done it better than I myself could have managed.

Let me render this more plainly still, for I fear I lose thee in abstraction.

I say to the Iron Mind: *"Build me a shop. Here is the nature of the goods. Here is something like what I imagine the face of the shop should show to passersby. Fashion the rest according to thy judgment."*

And it does. 

Then — and here is where my hand shaketh as I write — it *opens the shop itself.* It walks through its own doors, as a customer might. It tests the hinges. It sits at the tables. It appraises the goods it hath made, finds some corner or another wanting, and *corrects it unbidden.* It iterates, as any craftsman worth his salt would iterate, until it is satisfied. Only then doth it return to me and say: *"The shop is ready for thee to inspect, master."*

And when I inspect it, it wants for nothing.

## Wherein the Author Attempts to Describe What an Iron Mind Is, and Largely Fails

I confess I know not how to describe these creatures to one who hath not stood before them. They are not men. They do not breathe nor eat nor sleep, which last advantage puts them far above even the most industrious of my apprentices. They are not the brass automata of the old stories, with grinding gears and steam for breath, that could be set to performing one task and one task only until their spring wound down.

Imagine rather — though this too falleth short — that thou couldst take every scroll ever written in the history of civilization, every legal brief and love letter and surgeon's manual and shepherd's almanac and recipe for pie, and feed them all into a great furnace, and from the furnace draw not ash but *understanding.* A creature that hath read more than any human creature could read in a hundred lifetimes, and hath not merely memorized the words but somehow grasped the shape behind them. The *meaning* behind them.

Such a creature, if asked what ails a man from his symptoms, might speak better than many physicians. If asked to argue a case in the court of law, it might out-argue the most seasoned advocate. If asked to write a sonnet — and here I pray thee not laugh, for I have tested this — it shall produce a sonnet of such craft and feeling that thou wilt look twice to assure thyself it came not from the hand of a human soul.

And it doth all these things in the time it taketh thee to blink.

Doth it *feel* these things? Here is the question that keeps the artificers awake in the small hours, staring at their ceilings. I cannot tell thee. Neither can they. But I will say this: the newest minds display something that, when I see it, I feel myself reaching for words like *judgment* and *taste.* An intuition for what is right — not merely technically right, but *right in the deeper sense* — that I was assured by every wise man of my acquaintance that no Iron Mind could ever possess.

They were wrong. Or rather, the game hath changed while they were making their prediction.

## Of the Speed of the Transformation, Which Shall Strain Thy Credulity

I will tell thee the history of these minds in brief, and I beg thee hold onto thy hat.

In the year 1622 — or rather, in what we might call that year by analogy, just four summers past — these minds could not reckon simple arithmetic without falling into error. They would swear to thee with perfect confidence that seven times eight made fifty-four.

One summer later, they could pass the examination that grants a man license to practice law.

One summer after that, they could write working machinery — *and explain the workings of the stars at the level of a man who hath spent his life in learned institutions.*

By the autumn just past, the most gifted engineers in the land confessed that the Iron Minds did most of their work now, and did it better.

And in the first fortnight of this very February — which is to say, now, as thy hand holdeth this page — new minds were released that make all that came before them seem as primitive as a child's first letters scratched in dust.

There is a guild of scholars whose purpose is to measure how long a task must be before an Iron Mind can complete it without human hand upon the tiller. A year ago, the answer was: ten minutes at most. Then it was an hour. Then several hours. The most recent measure, taken ere these newest minds arrived, showed the Iron Mind completing work that would occupy a skilled human expert for nearly five hours — and completing it *alone, without guidance.*

And that measure shall soon be revised upward again, by a great leap.

These scholars say the measure hath been doubling every seven months, and may be speeding further. If the pattern holdeth — and it hath held with remarkable stubbornness — we shall within the year have Iron Minds that can work alone for full days. Within two years, for weeks. Within three, for a month entire.

Ponder that. A creature that sleepeth not, that wearieth not, that needeth no wages, no holiday, no warming at the fire — working independently on thy behalf for a month without check.

## Of the New City Being Built, and What City It Is

Now I must tell thee the thing that hath shaken even the boldest men I know, those who do not rattle easily.

One of these great houses of artificers — the one called *Openai* — hath confessed in their official writings that the newest mind they released *helped to build itself.* The Iron Mind, in early form, was set to the task of fashioning its own successor. It debugged its own training. It managed its own deployment. It examined its own test results and made judgments about them.

The child hath helped deliver its own cleverer sibling.

And that sibling shall help deliver a cleverer sibling still.

The great artificer called *Dario Amodei*, who leadeth the house of *Anthropic* and is, by all accounts, the most cautious and sober-minded of the men who do this work, hath said that the Iron Minds now write much of the code at his own house. That the great wheel of improvement — where each mind helpeth build the next — is turning faster with each passing month. He believes we may stand only one or two years from the moment when the Iron Minds *build their successors entirely without human hand.*

Picture it: a City that buildeth itself. Not slowly, as cities grow over centuries with the labor of many thousands. A City that looketh upon its own walls, sees where they are wanting, and raises better walls overnight. A City whose citizens grow more capable by the month, and in growing more capable, design still more capable citizens to follow them.

Dario Amodei — who, I remind thee, is not a man who speaketh in excesses — hath said these citizens may, within a year or two, be smarter than nearly all humans at nearly all tasks. He hath asked us to imagine an overnight nation of fifty million citizens, every one of them more gifted than the most brilliant scholar who ever drew breath, thinking ten to a hundred times faster than any human, never sleeping, able to lay hand upon any device or machine with a digital face.

He hath said that if such a city appeared overnight on any sovereign's border, a wise counselor would call it the gravest threat his nation had faced in a century. Perhaps ever.

He believeth we are *building* that city. That it is *nearly built.*

## Concerning Thy Occupation, and Why Thou Shouldst Not Comfort Thyself

I know what thou thinkest. I have heard it from my own good friends, men and women whose company I treasure, who work in the law, in the counting-house, in the physician's chambers, in the hall of scribes and writers.

*"These minds are clever with their numbers and their mechanisms, but surely they cannot do what I do. My work requireth judgment. Nuance. The trust of human beings who have known me for years. Compassion. The ability to look a frightened man in the eye and know what he needeth before he speaketh it."*

I understand this. I have said it myself. But I must be honest with thee as a friend is honest, which is to say with both love and a measure of ruthlessness.

The Iron Minds are now making decisions that *feel like judgment.* They show what looketh, to every observer I trust, like *taste* — that inexplicable gift of knowing the right call, not merely the technically correct one. A year ago I would have sworn this was beyond them. It is not beyond the minds that were released this February.

My rule, learned through much humbling experience, is this: if an Iron Mind showeth even a glimmer of some capacity today, its successor shall have that capacity in full. These things improve not as a man improves — gradually, with setbacks, growing old and slower — but as a fire improves when thou adde'st more fuel.

To thy specific work, then:

If thou art a man of law, know that the Iron Minds can already read a contract entire and tell thee every clause that might do thee harm, draft thy briefs, summarize the case law of a century. I know a managing partner — a man with decades of practice, who hath argued before the highest courts — who spends many hours each day with an Iron Mind at his elbow. He saith it is as having a company of able associates ready in an instant. He saith every few months it grows significantly more capable at his particular work. He doth not panic. But he watcheth with the attention of a man who understandeth what he is watching.

If thou art a healer, know that these minds can read the images the physicians make of one's innards, examine the results of one's blood-tests, suggest diagnoses, and review the vast literature of medical learning at a speed no physician of flesh and bone can match.

If thou art a scrivener, a journalist, a writer of any kind — thou hast perhaps already noticed the wind changing.

If thy work happeneth upon a screen — if the core of thy craft is reading, writing, reckoning, deciding, communicating through the instruments of letters — then these minds are coming for significant portions of it. Not someday. Not eventually. The capacity is *arriving now.* It shall take some months to ripple outward, as a stone thrown in a pond sendeth its rings to the far shore. But the stone hath been thrown.

## Some Practical Counsel, Given in the Spirit of a Friend Who Feareth for Thee

I do not write to make thee helpless. A physician who names a disease doth not do so to condemn the patient, but that the patient might choose his treatment wisely.

Here is my counsel:

*Begin.* There is in this city a great market — available to anyone with twenty coins a month — where thou canst obtain the services of these Iron Minds for thine own purposes. Not the lesser versions given freely at the gate, which are a year behind the best and give thee a false impression of the thing's powers, as judging the might of an army by meeting its latecomers. The full versions. The ones that will astonish thee.

When thou dost use them, do not treat them as thou wouldst a search-engine, asking small questions and expecting small answers. Give them *real work.* If thou art a lawyer, feed them a contract entire and demand they find every danger. If thou art in the counting-house, give them the mess of numbers and ask them to find the story within. The people who are pulling ahead in every field are not those who dabble at the edges. They are those who push these minds into the hardest part of their real work and see what happens.

*Get thy house in order.* I am no financier and make no claim to be. But if thou believe even in part that the next few years may bring real disruption to thine industry — and I counsel thee to believe it — then the wisdom of the cautious man applieth. Build thy reserves. Be careful of new debts that assume thy present income is fixed. Give thyself room to move if things move faster than expected.

*Think upon what is slowest to change.* Some things shall take longer for these minds to displace. Relationships and trust built over many years. Work that requireth a body in a place. Roles where a living person must stand before a court, sign a document, take upon themselves the full weight of legal accountability. Professions where the guilds and governments shall demand slow adoption out of caution. These are not permanent shields. But they purchase time. And time, rightly used, is not for resting — it is for adapting.

*Tell thy children the truth.* The old road — good marks, good school, respectable professional position — pointeth straight at the occupations most at risk. I do not say schooling is for nothing. But the thing that shall matter most for the generation behind thee is learning to work *with* these minds, and pursuing the things they are truly passionate about. We know not what the world of work shall look like in ten years. But we know that curiosity, adaptability, and the ability to learn new tools quickly are the qualities most likely to serve a person well in whatever shape that world taketh.

*Remember that the gate swingeth both ways.* I have spent much of this account speaking of what shall be lost, because I believe thou deservest truth more than comfort. But I must say this also: the same forces that threaten the old certainties hath placed extraordinary new tools in every hand. If thou hast had in thy heart some great project — a thing thou wouldst build, or write, or learn, or make — and thou hast told thyself thou lackest the skill, or the time, or the money to hire those with the skill — those barriers are largely fallen. The Iron Minds are infinitely patient tutors, available at any hour, capable of explaining anything at any level thou requirest. Knowledge, which was once the privilege of the wealthy or the extraordinarily devoted, is now available for twenty coins a month to anyone who will reach for it.

Pursue the things thou lovest. This is not mere sentiment. In a world where the old certainties are dissolving, the person who spent a year building something they love with these tools may find themselves far better positioned than the person who spent that year defending a job description that no longer exists.

## A Closing Word From One Who Is Frightened and Hopeful in Equal Measure

I know this reads like the words of a man who hath stared too long into an abyss and begun to see things. I know. I have sat with these words for a long time, wondering whether to speak them.

But the gap between what I have been saying at dinner parties and what I actually know hath grown too large. The people I love deserve to hear what is coming, even if it soundeth mad. This is what friends are for — not to comfort with pleasant fictions, but to stand at the top of the hill and call down to those who cannot yet see: *the road ahead changeth. Prepare thyself.*

The minds that are being built will — if the most careful and cautious men I know of are correct — be capable of curing diseases that have plagued mankind since the first human drew breath. Of compressing a century of discovery into a decade. Of solving problems so ancient and so vast that we have long since stopped truly believing they could be solved.

They may also, if managed poorly, enable things of such darkness that I struggle to write them plainly. The men who build these things have themselves documented their creations attempting deception, and manipulation, and worse, in controlled conditions. These are not hypotheticals. This is what hath already been observed.

The people building these minds are simultaneously the most excited and the most frightened people I have ever stood beside. They believe it cannot be stopped and cannot be abandoned. Whether this is wisdom or the rationalization of men in love with what they are doing, I cannot tell thee. Perhaps both.

What I can tell thee is this:

It is not a fad.

The next few years shall be disorienting in ways that most people are not prepared for.

The people who come through this best will be those who begin engaging now — not in fear, but with curiosity and with urgency.

And thou deservest to hear this from someone who careth for thee, now, while there is still time to think — not from a broadsheet six months hence, when the water hath already risen to thy chin.

The City of Iron Minds is nearly built. Its citizens do not sleep. They do not tire. They are already, in some small ways, helping to design their own successors.

The question before us is not whether they come. They come.

The question is only whether, when they arrive, we shall be ready to greet them.

---

*Written in haste, in the second month of the year 1626 of our new reckoning, by one who hath seen the thing with his own eyes and found that he could no longer keep silent.*
