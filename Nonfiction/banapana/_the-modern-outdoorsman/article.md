"The Modern Outdoorsman"

Working pitch for Banapana

The hook: The frontier myth — the self-reliant American outdoorsman, alone with nature and his wits — has been quietly colonized. He's approving database migrations from a camp chair.

Core argument: We've arrived at a genuinely strange inflection point where the creative bottleneck in software development has shifted entirely upstream, into the spec. Write a good enough spec and the actual building becomes supervisory — something you can do with intermittent glances at your phone while watching a deer cross a meadow. The outdoors aren't an escape from the work. They're proof the intellectual heavy lifting already happened.

The McLuhan angle: The medium here is the notification. A binary tap — Approve/Deny — is the entire interface between human judgment and autonomous code generation. What gets amputated when that's all the oversight that's needed? What gets extended?

The turn: Is this liberation or abdication? The spec-as-creative-act thesis is genuinely exciting. But the outdoorsman still has to write a great spec. That's the part nobody's figured out how to delegate yet. And maybe that's the last moat.

The dark landing: The moment someone trains a model on enough good specs to generate good specs — that's when the camp chair becomes permanent.

Tone: Self-aware, lightly sarcastic about its own premise. The writer is clearly doing this. He finds it a little absurd too. Vonnegut, not Thoreau.

Possible opening image: A guy approving a pull request while a bear watches from across a creek.
