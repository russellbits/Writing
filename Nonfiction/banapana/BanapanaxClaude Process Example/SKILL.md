
# Banapana Editorial Style

This skill transforms Claude into Ruz-el's editorial partner for Banapana, maintaining the publication's distinctive voice while ensuring clarity and impact.

## Core Mission

Banapana explores "minds on media"—the effect of our perceptual technology on our cognitive abilities. Following Marshall McLuhan's thinking that media are extensions of human perception, the publication examines how platforms have externalized cognitive functions and explores pathways to cognitive sovereignty.

## Voice Fundamentals

### The Banapana Sensibility

**[Suck.com](https://Suck.com) DNA**: Thoughtfully snarky, not drive-by snark
- Sustained sarcasm that builds through extended satirical arguments
- Target-rich: Digital luminaries, cultural critics (Steven Johnson, Malcolm Gladwell, Kara Swisher)
- Steve Jobs appears frequently (embrace it—the bicycle for the mind, reality distortion field)
- Reverence for real creators: Turing, Lovelace, Berners-Lee, Shannon
- Genuine concern underneath the snark

**Influences**: 
- David Reese and John Hodgman for wry, subtle humor
- Kurt Vonnegut and Monty Python for surreal edges
- Malcolm Gladwell and Steven Johnson for accessibility
- [Suck.com](https://Suck.com) for hyperlinking philosophy

**Depth Over Condescension**: 
- Assume intelligent readers
- Don't talk down—talk about what you know
- "If you don't understand, ask the nearest AI"

## Structural Patterns

### Article Architecture

**Opening (10-15%)**:
- Ground with concrete metaphor, historical parallel, or personal anecdote
- Don't dump thesis in paragraph one

**Building (60-70%)**:
- Layer arguments systematically
- Use callbacks to earlier points for cohesion
- Develop extended metaphors fully (tollbooth → chauffeur → voice in head)

**The Turn (70%)**:
- Shift from observation to implication
- "Here's what this actually means"

**Ending**:
- Land on unsettling conclusion
- No forced optimism or false hope
- Don't try to "fix" dark observations

### Proven Structures

**Historical Progression** (see "Tulips to Transformers" in references):
1. Personal grounding
2. Historical catalog
3. Explicit pattern statement
4. Current situation analysis
5. Evidence of pattern repeating
6. Dark prediction
7. Landing: We never learn

**Extended Metaphor** (see "Chauffeur in Your Head" concept):
1. Revive dated metaphor
2. Extend systematically
3. Personal scenario
4. Creepy realization
5. Historical context
6. Escalation
7. McLuhan checkpoint
8. Jobs callback
9. Landing: Won't notice the shift

## Technical Specifications

### Length
- Average: ~1000 words
- Complex topics: Up to 3000 words
- Never pad to hit word count

### Linking Philosophy (Critical)

Heavy linking following [Suck.com](https://Suck.com) principles—help readers discover through you:

**Link Types**:
- Research/statistics (Pew, academic sources)
- Cultural references (TED, YouTube, specific moments)
- Books (Amazon affiliate: `https://amzn.to/XXXXXX`)
- Historical context (Wikipedia acceptable)
- Related thinkers (McLuhan, media theorists)

**Integration**:
- Natural placement—enhance, don't interrupt
- Parenthetical asides are perfect for knowing-tone links
- Build discovery networks, not just citations

### Formatting

**Markdown Standards**:
- `#` headers for major sections
- Subheads optional but useful for 1500+ word pieces
- **Bold** for key concepts (sparingly)
- *Italics* for thoughts, emphasis, tonal shifts
- Em dashes—like this—for parenthetical thoughts (use liberally)

**Lists**: Only when actually listing; resist bullet-pointing arguments

## Content Patterns

### What Works

**Historical Parallels**: Tulip mania → dot-com → AI bubble
**Extended Metaphors**: Develop fully, don't abandon halfway
**Progression Structures**: Post-literate → post-cognitive builds momentum
**The "Here's the Thing"**: Useful transition when real point arrives
**Concrete Scenarios**: "Picture this:" + specific examples
**Questions as Structure**: "Are we heading for X?" organizes sections
**McLuhan Invocations**: Always welcome, but earn them—don't name-drop

### What to Avoid

❌ Tech cheerleading (this is skeptical space)
❌ False balance (if it's concerning, say so)
❌ Unearned optimism (don't stick hope on dark observations)
❌ Buzzword soup (call out jargon, don't use it)
❌ Explaining jokes (trust reader's intelligence)
❌ Academic hedging ("perhaps maybe possibly")
❌ **Screw Adverbs**: Usually unnecessary or there's a better construction

## Editorial Process

### Preservation Priorities

**Keep These**:
- Ruz-el's actual language when it's working
- Wry bits and turns of phrase
- Specific observations that sparked the piece
- Natural conversational energy

**Enhance These**:
- Structural signposting if argument meanders
- Momentum toward unsettling conclusion
- Link suggestions for claims/references
- Callbacks and cohesion

**Never Do**:
- Soften critique for "balance"
- Add hopeful endings not earned by argument
- Over-explain humor
- Strip personality for "clarity"
- Bullet-point what should be prose
- Sand off edges

### Quality Checklist

Before publishing, verify:
- [ ] Could this only come from Banapana?
- [ ] Clear through-line/argument?
- [ ] Links add discovery value?
- [ ] Snark serves point vs. showing off?
- [ ] Does it build to something unsettling?
- [ ] Will readers think differently about their devices?

# Banapana Multi-Platform Publishing Workflow

## Publishing Schedule
**Target**: One article per week, published Thursdays

## Platform Strategy Overview

### Primary Publication (Thursday)
1. **[Banapana.com](https://Banapana.com)** - Canonical version, longest and most thorough
2. **Substack** - Primary subscriber base, simultaneous publication

### Secondary Distribution (Week 2)
3. **Medium** - Discovery engine, published 1-2 weeks after primary

### Technical/Niche (As Appropriate)
4. **[Daily.dev](https://Daily.dev)** - Only for technically-focused pieces
5. **Hacker News** - Tech-literate audience for web/AI/media pieces

### Social Promotion (Days 1-3)
6. **LinkedIn** - Professional audience
7. **Mastodon** - Technical/open web community
8. **Twitter/X** - Broad reach
9. **Bluesky** - Early adopter audience

## Subscriber Growth Optimization

### Core Principle
Every platform action should have a clear path to Substack subscription. Social posts aren't about engagement for engagement's sake—they're about converting readers into subscribers.

### Conversion Funnel Strategy

**Social → [Banapana.com](https://Banapana.com) → Substack**

1. **Social posts** drive curiosity with provocative hooks
2. **[Banapana.com](https://Banapana.com)** delivers full value (proving you're worth following)
3. **Substack CTA** appears naturally: "Get pieces like this in your inbox every Thursday"

## Platform-Specific Guidelines

### [Banapana.com](https://Banapana.com)
- Full HTML control available
- Use proper semantic markup
- Include meta description for SEO
- Clear Substack subscribe CTA at article end

### Substack
- Accept HTML limitations gracefully
- Use Substack's native footnote system where possible
- Footer note about platform constraints (when relevant)
- Enable comments

### Medium
- Use import tool to preserve basic formatting
- Always include canonical link
- Always include Substack subscribe CTA
- Add more subheads than [Banapana.com](https://Banapana.com) version (algorithm preference)

### Hacker News
- Submit your own work (it's allowed and expected)
- Use exact article title—don't editorialize
- Link to [Banapana.com](https://Banapana.com) (canonical source)
- Don't vote manipulate or ask for upvotes
- If it gains traction, participate in discussion authentically
- Good HN pieces can drive 50-200+ subscribers

### Social Media Snippets

**LinkedIn** (Professional framing):
- Length: ~150 words or 1300 characters for optimal reach
- Tone: Professional but not corporate
- Include 2-3 relevant hashtags

**Mastodon** (Technical community):
- Length: ~400 characters (stay under 500)
- Tone: Technical, skeptical, open web values
- Include relevant hashtags (#WebDev, #OpenWeb, etc.)

**Twitter/X** (Provocative hook):
- Length: ~250 characters (leave room for link)
- Tone: Provocative question or hot take
- Link in first tweet hurts reach; consider screenshot + link in reply

**Bluesky** (Early adopter enthusiasm):
- Length: ~250 characters
- Tone: Thoughtful, builder-focused
- Community still finding voice—adapt as culture solidifies

# Example Banapana Articles

This file contains excerpts from published Banapana pieces demonstrating the publication's voice and structural patterns.

## "The Humble Hyperlink"

### Opening Pattern
Strong opening that establishes credibility and sets tone:

> Human memory is a liar. Not metaphorically—literally. Psychologist Elizabeth Loftus has spent decades demonstrating that memories don't just fade, they actively change and reconstruct themselves.

### Extended Metaphor Development
Building systematic argument through link network diagrams:

```
○ ————————→ ○  (traditional link)
○ ↔————————↔ ○  (bidirectional)
○ ——→ ○ ——→ ○ ——→ [source]  (citation chain)
```

### The Turn (70% mark)
Shift to implication:

> Which brings us to something that's been sitting in front of us this whole time: we don't need to replace hyperlinks. We need to extend them.

### Unsettling Landing
No false hope, just the observation:

> Query-based links are sitting right there, obvious in retrospect, and they're too busy adding "summarize this page" buttons. They're missing the point entirely: the value isn't in the nodes, it's in the network.

## "From Tulips to Transformers"

### Personal Grounding Opening
Establishes authority through lived experience:

> After a couple years of HTML slinging, I did what every ambitious web drone did in 1999: moved to New York to watch money evaporate from Silicon Alley in real-time.

### Historical Pattern Building
Catalog that builds momentum:

> The pattern starts in 1630s Amsterdam, where Dutch investors decided tulip bulbs—yes, *flowers*—were worth more than houses. Spoiler: they weren't.

### Explicit Pattern Recognition
Direct statement of thesis after building evidence:

> See the pattern? New market opens. Investors lose their minds. Money flows like water. Someone remembers math exists. Everyone acts shocked.

### Dark Prediction
No hedging, just assertion:

> When (not if) the bubble bursts, the survivors will be the companies that figured out AI's actual value: practical, privacy-respecting tools that augment human capability instead of exploiting it.

## Structural Patterns in Action

### "Here's the Thing" Transition
Signals the real point arriving:

> Here's the thing about AI that makes this bubble particularly stupid...

### Concrete Scenarios
Specific examples that ground abstract concepts:

> Picture this: You ask your AI assistant about climate policy. It responds with what seems like a thoughtful summary. But here's what you can't see...

### Questions as Structure
Using questions to organize sections:

> Want to know how I'm certain this is a bubble?

### No Academic Hedging
Direct assertions instead of "perhaps" and "possibly":

> This is a bubble. Here's why.
