Here are five especially egregious Web-era claims from the 1999–2001 dot-com bubble that were plainly wrong in hindsight and fit the “say it big to raise capital” pattern:

| Claim | Who said it | Why it was wrong |
|---|---|---|
| Internet traffic was “doubling every 100 days.” | WorldCom/UUNet executives and the claim was repeated by the U.S. government in 1998. | Later reporting showed the real growth rate was far slower—roughly doubling about once a year after the mid-1990s surge.  [bizjournals](https://www.bizjournals.com/sanfrancisco/stories/1998/04/13/daily23.html) |
| Online grocery would become a winner-take-all, scale-driven business that could beat supermarkets on price and convenience. | Webvan executives. | Webvan shut down in 2001, showing that the model’s economics and demand assumptions were badly overstated.  [knowledge.wharton.upenn](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/podcast/knowledge-at-wharton-podcast/what-webvan-could-have-learned-from-tesco/) |
| People would do most shopping online by the mid-2000s. | Widely repeated by dot-com-era executives and boosters, including major internet-era prognosticators. | Even strong e-commerce growth did not make “most shopping” online by that date; the claim badly overestimated consumer adoption.  [bus.umich](https://www.bus.umich.edu/KresgePublic/Journals/EmarketerReports/2003/North%20America%20eCommerce%20B2B%20and%20B2C%20Report%20May%202003.pdf) |
| Strip malls were basically finished. | Jeff Bezos, in a 1999 prediction. | Strip malls did not disappear; physical retail remained central for decades, even as e-commerce grew.  [cnbc](https://www.cnbc.com/2017/08/18/jeff-bezos-made-eerily-accurate-predictions-in-1999.html) |
| Online grocers would quickly take over routine grocery buying. | The broader online-grocery industry pitch, reflected in 1999 coverage and company messaging. | The dot-com-era online grocery sector mostly imploded in 2000–2001, with only a few survivors and much slower adoption than promised.  [latimes](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1999-may-24-fi-40411-story.html) |

## Why these stand out
These weren’t just optimistic forecasts; they were the kinds of statements that implied unavoidable, near-term dominance, which made them useful for attracting investors during the bubble. The pattern was especially clear in telecom and e-commerce, where executives could point to explosive-sounding metrics or “inevitable” consumer behavior to justify huge spending and valuations. [lightreading](https://www.lightreading.com/business-management/did-worldcom-puff-up-the-internet-too-)

## Most defensible “top 5”
If you want the five most outrageous in pure hindsight-value, I’d rank them as:
1. WorldCom’s “doubling every 100 days” traffic claim. [bizjournals](https://www.bizjournals.com/sanfrancisco/stories/1998/04/13/daily23.html)
2. Webvan’s expectation that it could outcompete supermarkets on a new logistics stack. [knowledge.wharton.upenn](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/podcast/knowledge-at-wharton-podcast/what-webvan-could-have-learned-from-tesco/)
3. The claim that online shopping would dominate by the mid-2000s. [bus.umich](https://www.bus.umich.edu/KresgePublic/Journals/EmarketerReports/2003/North%20America%20eCommerce%20B2B%20and%20B2C%20Report%20May%202003.pdf)
4. Bezos’s “strip malls are history” prediction. [cnbc](https://www.cnbc.com/2017/08/18/jeff-bezos-made-eerily-accurate-predictions-in-1999.html)
5. The broader online-grocery hype that assumed rapid mass adoption and easy profitability. [latimes](https://www.latimes.com/archives/la-xpm-1999-may-24-fi-40411-story.html)
