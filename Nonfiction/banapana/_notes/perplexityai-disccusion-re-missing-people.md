### How often do hikers go missing?

There isn’t a single official annual number just for “hikers,” but some useful proxies:

- The National Park Service region–wide reports roughly **1,000–2,500 people reported missing per year** across U.S. national parks alone, and many of these are hikers or people in the backcountry. [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9cm6OQwGYRk)
- A broader U.S. wilderness‑search estimate tied to Yosemite‑style SAR data implies on the order of **tens of thousands of lost‑person incidents per year** nationwide, with the vast majority resolved relatively quickly. [thecoldcases](https://www.thecoldcases.com/p/missing-hikers-across-america-stories)
- Nationally, **over 600,000 people are reported missing each year** in all circumstances in the U.S., with about 90% recovered within a year; tens of thousands remain missing longer‑term. [strangeoutdoors](https://www.strangeoutdoors.com/questions-answers/national-park-stats)

So: **hikers going missing is not rare in absolute terms**, and it can look “pattern‑like” when you cherry‑pick a few high‑profile cases, even if they are, statistically, just tail‑end events in a large dataset.

### How to interpret this

- **Confirmed pattern:** Two people (Reza and McCasland) disappeared on hiking‑type outings; one (Loureiro) was fatally shot; one (Casias) went missing; and one or more others are sometimes shoehorned in. None of these are tied to UAPs in any official, evidentiary way.  
- **Speculative overlay:** The “five missing/murdered scientists tied to UAP” narrative is a **journalistic/Internet‑story amalgam**, not a documented phenomenon. It uses real, tragic cases but arranges them into a pattern that higher‑quality reporting does not corroborate. Me:
"Doing it on two phones and then discarding them (or leaving them reset at home) could, in theory, just be clumsy behavior or someone trying to avoid tracking." Sure. But then why avoid being tracked?

Perplexity:
Exactly—that’s the crux of the weirdness, and where the “normal” framing starts to fray.

If someone with a tech background suddenly:
- walks away from home,  
- leaves clothes behind,  
- **and** resets two phones so they’re effectively “blank”  

…then the plausible explanations fall into a few camps, from mundane to darker:

### 1. “Avoiding tracking” for benign reasons
- They might have been trying to:
  - Drop off the grid temporarily (mid‑life crisis, mental‑health spiral, desire for anonymity).  
  - Prevent family or employers from monitoring their movements.  
- In that case, wiping the phones would be a **premeditated step toward privacy**, not random clumsiness.  
- This would still be an odd, atypical *mix* of behaviors for a 68‑year‑old retired general, but not necessarily malign.

### 2. Someone else trying to avoid tracking
- If the person didn’t reset the phones themselves, then:
  - Someone may have taken them, wiped them, and left them at the house.  
  - That would strongly suggest **third‑party involvement** (criminal, hack-and‑hide, or some kind of op‑type cleanup).  
- In that case, the “avoid tracking” motive transfers from the missing person to **whoever handled the phones afterward**.

### 3. Derangement or distress, not logic
- If the person was in acute mental or emotional distress (episode, paranoia, mania), they might:
  - Wipe phones compulsively, almost ritualistically, as part of “cutting ties.”  
  - Still be trying to “avoid tracking,” but less from a cold, strategic plan and more from a disturbed state.  
- That’s still weird behavior, but it doesn’t require a conspiracy to explain—just a **serious mental‑health or crisis event**.

### Why your question hits the nerve

Your skepticism is on point because:
- “Why avoid being tracked?” only really makes sense if:
  - someone has something to hide (or fears being watched), or  
  - someone else *wants* to hide the fact that they were there.  
- In a “normal” hiking‑disappearance case, you’d usually see **no evidence of deliberate phone‑wiping**; the fact that there are **two wiped phones** pushes the narrative away from “oops, wandered off” and toward “something intentional happened with the devices.”

So to answer your question directly:  
They *might* avoid being tracked because they’re trying to go off‑grid, or because someone else is trying to obscure their trail.  
The ambiguity is precisely why calling this a “normal” missing‑person case feels off—**the behavior around the phones is abnormal, even if we don’t yet know *why*.**

Perplexity:

Yes—this sounds like you’re mixing two semi‑famous “weird‑cave” disappearances in the Southwest, one of which is **not** in New Mexico but often gets blended into regional lore.

The story you’re thinking of is almost certainly **Kenny Veach**, the YouTube‑explorer who vanished in the Nevada desert in 2014, but whose myth often spills over into New‑Mexico‑style “demon cave” legends.

### Kenny Veach and the “M‑Cave”

- Kenny Veach was a self‑taught explorer and weapons inventor who lived in Las Vegas and became obsessed with a mysterious **“M‑shaped cave”** in the Mojave Desert near the Nellis Air Force Range (close to Area‑51‑adjacent territory).  
- He described feeling intense vibrations and “something not right” when he briefly approached the cave, which he associated with strange energies or even paranormal activity.  
- In November 2014 he told his girlfriend and online audience he was going back to search for the cave alone; he left a message saying, essentially, “if I don’t come back, search for me.”  
- He never returned. Searchers found some of his **gear and vehicles** in the desert, but his body has never been found.  
- Over time, the “M‑Cave” has picked up all kinds of labels: **portal, government tunnel, demon‑vault, reptile‑lair, etc.**—none of which are substantiated, but they’re why your brain latched on to “man obsessed with a demon cave in the desert.”

### Why it feels like New Mexico

Even though Veach’s case is in **Nevada**, it often gets folded into:
- broader “desert‑mystery” lore (including Chaco Canyon, Skinwalker‑type stories, and military‑base rumors),  
- and the **New‑Mexico/UFO** narrative because the geography and culture are similar, and the “secret caves demons” trope is the same.

So your memory is basically right:  
- Guy obsessed with a strange desert cave he associates with weird energies/demons.  
- He goes back to find it.  
- They find his equipment, but **he vanishes**.  
That’s Kenny Veach’s story, not a New‑Mexico‑specific case, but very much the kind of “cave‑demon” urban legend you’re recalling.
