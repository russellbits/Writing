# The Therapist Will Algorithm Now (Some)

Chicago emergency rooms use algorithms to decide whether heart patients get admitted or sent home. Input the [HEART Score variables](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9635776/)—patient history, ECG readings, age, risk factors, troponin levels—and out comes a number. A six means moderate risk: admit for observation. A two means you're probably fine: here's your discharge paperwork and some lifestyle advice about sodium.

The algorithm doesn't replace the cardiologist's judgment. It standardizes the thinking that happens before judgment enters the picture. It ensures that at 3 AM on a Tuesday, with the ER overflowing and everyone exhausted, the same risk calculation happens as would at 10 AM on a quiet Wednesday with fresh coffee and adequate staffing.

Meanwhile, [28% of people have used AI chatbots](https://news.llu.edu/health-wellness/can-i-use-ai-my-therapist-truth-about-turning-chatbots-therapy) for "quick support and as a personal therapist." [ChatGPT has nearly 700 million weekly users](https://www.npr.org/sections/shots-health-news/2025/09/30/nx-s1-5557278/ai-artificial-intelligence-mental-health-therapy-chatgpt-openai)—and while OpenAI won't say how many are using it for therapy, people absolutely are. They're trauma-dumping to LLMs with no crisis protocols, no escalation pathways, no evidence-based frameworks. Just whatever the model happens to generate based on its training data and the vibes of the conversation.

This is already happening. The question isn't whether AI will be used for mental health support—it's whether anyone with actual clinical expertise will standardize what that looks like.

Now imagine taking the same approach as those ER algorithms—the Model Context Protocol server, to use the technical term—and pointing it at cognitive behavioral therapy.

## The CBT Black Box

Here's what makes therapists nervous: CBT is *suspiciously algorithmic*. You identify cognitive distortions (catastrophizing, black-and-white thinking, overgeneralization). You challenge them with Socratic questions. You design behavioral experiments. You assign homework. You track symptoms with standardized instruments like the PHQ-9 or GAD-7. 

It's not that CBT *is* an algorithm—the therapeutic relationship, the attunement, the knowing when to push and when to hold space, all that matters enormously. But significant chunks of it could be codified. Professional organizations could get together and build an MCP server that provides:

- `identify_cognitive_distortions()` 
- `suggest_socratic_questions()`
- `create_behavioral_experiment()`
- `assess_treatment_progress()`
- `check_safety_protocols()`

Feed it patient data, get back evidence-based interventions. Update it when new research changes best practices. Any MCP-compatible AI could access it. Instant standardization of therapeutic protocols across every chatbot claiming to offer mental health support.

Which is either brilliant or terrifying, depending on whether you're paying for therapy or providing it.

## The Thing We're Not Saying

The resistance to this idea—and there would be *considerable* resistance—isn't really about whether it's technically possible. It's about what it implies: **therapists are partially replaceable**.

Not entirely. Not for complex cases, not for the relational magic that happens in good therapy, not for the human capacity to read what's *not* being said. But for psychoeducation? Teaching someone what cognitive distortions are? Generating thought record worksheets? Tracking symptoms week to week? 

An algorithm can do that. Research shows that structured self-help CBT—even just *workbooks*—produces outcomes comparable to therapist-delivered CBT for mild-to-moderate depression and anxiety. Internet-based CBT programs work. Chatbots work well enough that millions of people are already using them, professional standards be damned.

So the threat isn't hypothetical. It's economic. If CBT can be reduced to a protocol, insurance companies will absolutely use that to justify lower reimbursement rates. "Why pay $150 per session when there's an app for $15 a month?" The market pressure isn't about whether therapists provide value—it's about whether they provide *enough* value to justify the cost differential.

And here's the uncomfortable part: for some people, at some levels of severity, maybe they don't. [A 2024 study found that therapists couldn't reliably tell the difference between human-human therapy transcripts and human-AI therapy transcripts](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10447318.2024.2385001)—they were accurate only 53.9% of the time, no better than chance. They even rated the AI transcripts as higher quality on average. AI-based conversational agents reduced depression symptoms by 64% in a systematic review of over 3,800 participants.

The evidence is mounting that for mild-to-moderate conditions, structured digital interventions *work*. Not for everyone. Not for complex cases. But for a meaningful subset of people who need help.

## The Escalator, Not the Replacement

But there's a smarter way to think about this. Not "algorithm versus therapist" but **tiered care with clear escalation pathways**.

**Level 1**: Psychoeducation chatbot. Basic CBT concepts, mood tracking, journaling prompts. For general wellness, mild stress, learning coping skills. Referral triggers: persistent symptoms beyond two weeks, functional impairment, user request.

**Level 2**: Structured protocol chatbot. Full CBT protocols for specific conditions—social anxiety, insomnia, mild depression. For diagnosed mild-to-moderate conditions in motivated users. Referral triggers: no improvement after 4-6 weeks, symptom worsening, crisis indicators.

**Level 3**: Human-supervised AI. Chatbot assists, licensed therapist reviews sessions and adjusts approach. For moderate symptoms, complex presentations, those needing accountability. Referral triggers: severe symptoms, suicide ideation, trauma, personality disorders.

**Level 4**: Full human therapy. Traditional therapist-led care. For severe or complex cases, those who failed lower levels, crisis situations.

The MCP server's most important function in this model isn't delivering CBT techniques—it's **knowing when to escalate**. PHQ-9 score above 15? Move up a level. User expresses suicidal ideation with a plan? Immediate crisis protocols. No improvement after eight weeks of structured self-help? Time for human intervention.

This isn't about replacing therapists. It's about not wasting their expertise on cases that don't require it. It's about having *something* available for the millions of people who can't access or afford traditional therapy. It's about meeting people where they are instead of forcing everyone through the same expensive, waitlist-laden system.

## The Referral Problem

Here's where most of these systems fail: the handoff. User reaches the limit of what the chatbot can handle, gets a phone number for a crisis line or a list of therapists accepting new patients, and... nothing happens. The waitlist is three months long. They don't have insurance. It feels like rejection—"even the robot gave up on me."

A proper MCP server architecture would handle this better. It schedules the appointment *for* the user. It provides interim support while they're waiting. It explains why escalation is recommended: not failure, but appropriate matching of resources to need. It packages everything the chatbot learned and sends it to the human clinician so nobody starts from scratch.

The warm handoff. The continuity of care. The thing that keeps people from falling through the cracks between levels.

## What We're Really Automating

We're not automating therapy—we're automating *triage*. The chatbot isn't replacing the therapist any more than the telephone replaced face-to-face conversation. It's extending our reach, creating a new kind of access, changing who gets helped and how quickly.

What do we lose when some therapeutic functions get algorithmic? The serendipity of the wrong question leading to the right breakthrough. The way a skilled therapist picks up on the tiny hesitation that reveals everything. The permission to be messy and contradictory that you don't get from a system optimized for protocol adherence.

We're not having a debate about whether this *should* happen. People are already using ChatGPT for therapy. They're trauma-dumping to chatbots that have no crisis protocols, no escalation pathways, no evidence-based protocols—just vibes and whatever the LLM happens to generate. 

The question is whether the mental health profession will lead in creating standards for this, or watch from the sidelines while tech companies define what "AI therapy" means.

## The Part Nobody Wants to Admit

Therapists provide immense value. They're also, for a certain subset of cases at a certain level of severity, doing work that doesn't require their full training and expertise. That's not an insult—it's an observation about resource allocation in a world where mental health needs vastly outstrip mental health resources.

The tiered system admits this. It says: yes, parts of what we do *can* be standardized and delivered at scale. And by acknowledging that, by building the MCP server ourselves with proper safeguards and escalation protocols, we ensure that when automation happens—and it is happening—it happens ethically. With professional oversight. With clear boundaries about what's appropriate for a chatbot versus what requires human clinical judgment.

It's the difference between building the highway system and just letting everyone drive wherever. One creates infrastructure that serves more people safely. The other creates chaos and casualties.

The HEART Score doesn't threaten cardiologists. It makes triage faster and more consistent so cardiologists can focus on complex cases. A CBT MCP server could do the same for mental health—if we can get past the fear that standardizing something makes it worthless.

The algorithm doesn't judge your worthiness for care. It doesn't have ego invested in being irreplaceable. It just asks: does this person need help? What level of help? How do we get them there?

Maybe that's not such a terrible thing to standardize.
