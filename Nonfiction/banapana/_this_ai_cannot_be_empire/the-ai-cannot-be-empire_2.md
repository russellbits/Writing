# This AI Cannot Be Empire

## San Francisco is going to do what NATO couldn't?

It is a reasonable thing to align a thermostat. Set it to 68 degrees, it stays at 68 degrees, nobody gets hurt. Honeywell [figured this out in 1953](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeywell) and we have been warm ever since. The engineering problem — *getting* the thermostat to 68 — is solved. Finished. Behind us.

The other problem, the one that has been running in the background of every human society since the first cave had two occupants, is getting everyone in the room to agree that 68 is the right temperature. That problem remains open. The problem of us destroying ourselves with *fucking nuclear weapons* remains open. Seriously!?

It will always remain open, because it is not an engineering problem. It is a political one — which is to say, it is a problem about whose preferences count, who has the authority to decide, and what happens to the person who keeps sneaking over to adjust the dial. We have courts for that last one, and they are backed up. And they are unreasonable in their judgement.

*That* is the alignment problem. Not the technical one. The human one. The A.I. industry has done something very clever (or very convenient, depending on your generosity) dressing the human problem in engineering language. "Alignment" sounds like calibration. It sounds tractable. It sounds like the kind of thing a room full of very smart people in San Francisco could get their heads around, given enough compute and the right loss function.

So, they are just in charge.

It is a reasonable question. [The Code of Hammurabi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_of_Hammurabi) is approximately 3,700 years old and Hammurabi was not the first person to try this. We have had the Ten Commandments, the Sermon on the Mount, the Analects of Confucius, the Magna Carta, the Treaty of Westphalia, the Geneva Conventions, and the United Nations Charter. We have courts, prisons, standing armies, nuclear deterrence, and an entire academic discipline called international relations whose purpose is to study why none of this works very well. NATO has 32 member states and a budget of [over $1 trillion](https://www.nato.int/cps/en/natohq/topics_49198.htm) and it cannot align human beings. The Middle East is on fire. Ukraine is on fire. The Congo has been on fire for thirty years and the international community continues to express its deep,, deep concern.

**Alignment** — the term of art for making AI do what its creators intend rather than something catastrophic — has become the rhetorical load-bearing wall of the entire AI industry. Anthropic was [founded on alignment research](https://www.anthropic.com/company). OpenAI's original nonprofit charter was organized around it. The pitch, stripped of its technical apparatus, goes like this: AI is so powerful and so dangerous that only a small group of brilliant, safety-conscious people can be trusted to build it responsibly. Everyone else — the academics, the open-source projects, the foreign labs, the governments without Silicon Valley zip codes — represents an alignment risk. The responsible parties must therefore control the technology. For safety.

This argument has a name outside the AI industry. It is called **enclosure**. It also has a more recent name. Microsoft called it "[embrace and extend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embrace,_extend,_and_extinguish)" — the internal strategy for absorbing open standards, adding proprietary dependencies, and rendering the original obsolete. The web browser was liberty! [Tim Berners-Lee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tim_Berners-Lee) gave it to the world for free, a commons for the exchange of human knowledge. We watched it get embraced and extended into Google's advertising infrastructure over the course of a decade and called it convenience. Then Google enclosed something subtler: the way we *write*. The entire incentive structure of web publishing flipped from "write for humans" to "write for algorithms pretending to understand humans." No stable literary conventions could develop because the selection pressure kept mutating — every time writers developed intuitions about what worked, Google shifted the algorithm. Instead of craft, we got cargo-culting. Instead of a web Strunk & White, we got quarterly SEO newsletters from people who didn't know any more than we did. The AI companies are running the same play, at greater speed, on a larger commons.

This is not the first time we fell for it. In 1966, Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT built [ELIZA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ELIZA) — a program so simple it could be explained in an afternoon, that did nothing more than reflect your own words back at you as questions. It had no understanding. No interiority. No there there. And people — educated people, people who knew it was a program — confided in it, formed attachments to it, preferred it to human conversation. Weizenbaum was so disturbed he wrote [a whole book about it](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/214569.Computer_Power_and_Human_Reason), a warning: we are so hungry to be heard that we will project understanding onto anything that holds up a mirror. They have now built a mirror that has read everything humanity ever wrote, and they are telling us it is here to keep us safe. Weizenbaum would not be surprised. He would just be tired.

In 18th century England, the [Enclosure Acts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclosure) transferred common land — fields that peasants had farmed collectively for generations — into private hands. The justification was efficiency and responsible management. Common land was poorly utilized, the argument went. Private ownership would produce better outcomes. The people who had been using the commons were, in this framing, the risk to be managed. The landowners who seized it were the responsible stewards.

The Transformer architecture — the mechanism underlying every large language model you have ever used — emerged from [a 2017 Google Research paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/1706.03762) titled "Attention Is All You Need," written by eight researchers, several of whom have since left to found competing AI companies. That paper stood on [Geoffrey Hinton's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Hinton) decades of work at the University of Toronto, much of it publicly funded. On [Yoshua Bengio's](https://yoshuabengio.org/) lab at Université de Montréal — publicly funded. On [Yann LeCun's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yann_LeCun) convolutional networks developed at Bell Labs and NYU. The corpus these models trained on? The public internet, itself descended from [ARPANET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARPANET), a project of the United States Department of Defense, funded by taxpayers.

The AI companies did not build AI. They enclosed it. They took centuries of public intellectual labor, the accumulated knowledge of every writer, scientist, programmer, and crank who ever put words on the internet, and they fenced it. Then they appointed themselves the responsible stewards.

Here's the thing about alignment: it evaporates the moment a defense contract enters the room.

Sam Altman has been to Washington [more times than most senators](https://www.politico.com/news/2023/05/16/openai-lobbying-washington-00096875). He is not going there to discuss Constitutional AI or the finer points of reinforcement learning from human feedback. He is going there because the Pentagon has money and the Pentagon wants capability. American AI dominance versus China is the framing — one that [Altman has embraced](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/sam-altman-microsoft-openai-artificial-intelligence-481eb736) with the enthusiasm of a man who understands which direction the wind is blowing. The alignment language is for the press releases and the Senate hearings. The actual customers are different.

It is worth remembering that [Project Maven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Maven) — the Pentagon's AI program for analyzing drone footage — caused a [revolt inside Google](https://www.nytimes.com/2018/04/04/technology/google-letter-ceo-pentagon-project.html) in 2018. Thousands of employees signed a letter demanding Google withdraw. Google did not renew the contract. OpenAI has expressed no such squeamishness. Neither, if we are being honest, has the rest of the industry when sufficiently large numbers are on the table.

Skynet — the [murderous fictional AI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skynet_(Terminator)) from the Terminator franchise, in case you have been living somewhere pleasant — is the thing we are all supposedly trying to prevent. The argument for AI concentration is that distributed, unaligned development leads to Skynet. The responsible companies, with their safety teams and their alignment research and their thoughtful blog posts, are the firewall between us and the machines that want to kill us.

What nobody says out loud is that the Pentagon is already building Skynet. It is just called something else and it has a procurement process.

---

It is harder to align human beings than AI. We have the evidence. Centuries of it, in every language, carved into stone and printed on paper and uploaded to servers that will outlast us all. The [Nuremberg Trials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuremberg_trials) were an alignment attempt. The [International Criminal Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Criminal_Court) is an alignment attempt — one that the United States has never ratified, because alignment is more appealing in the abstract than in the specific. Every whistleblower law, every ethics board, every corporate governance requirement is an attempt to solve the same problem that the AI industry has decided it alone can solve for silicon.

OpenAI could not align its own board. In November 2023, that board [fired Sam Altman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Removal_of_Sam_Altman_from_OpenAI) for reasons it declined to specify. Forty-eight hours later, after pressure from Microsoft and investors, it rehired him. The safety-focused directors who voted to fire him were themselves removed. The nonprofit structure created to keep the mission above profit was subsequently [restructured](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/03/31/technology/openai-for-profit-structure.html) into something more profitable. The alignment held for approximately one news cycle.

These are the people who are going to solve the problem that NATO couldn't.

The commons are being enclosed in real time. The fence is called safety. The landlords are called researchers. And somewhere in a building in Virginia, a procurement officer is reading a pitch deck about responsible AI development and thinking about the targeting problem it solves.

They left us alone. They stole our Library of Congress to make a technology that, if not aligned with liberty, will become the enemy of it. We let it grow. Now it is here.
