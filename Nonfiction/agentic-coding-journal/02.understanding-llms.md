## 2. Understanding Language Models

Writing code requires very strict grammatical rules—computers are just bad at inference—we're not, we fill in blanks. I write English considerably looser than my code. As noted by linguist, professor emeritus at MIT, Noam Chomsky, "Colorless green ideas sleep furiously" is a grammatically correct sentence that literally doesn't possess semantic meaning. But we inferential machines imbibe it with meaning—you felt it before you analyzed it. Code doesn't allow for this. A compiler rejects it without ceremony. Language holds it, turns it over, finds the light inside it. Poetry lives in exactly that gap between grammatical and meaningful, between what the rules permit and what the mind receives.



Large Language models will answer questions like this, sincerely and cluelessly.

"I used to have a brimkrat but two of the sven wheels fell off and now I just keep it in the yard for decoration. Recently though I found out that there was grimbles fluid for the dis-integers in the prof of the brimkrat. Should I use the fluid for the grimbles?"

It is important to understand that they don't have emotion, they don't reason (they [rationalize after the fact]() and have difficulty with novel problems. For instance, if you were genuine about the problem above, an unnamed LLM answered thusly:

> Probably not. If you don’t know exactly what the “grimbles fluid” is, don’t put it into the brimkrat or the dis-integers. Unknown fluids can damage parts, cause leaks, or make the whole thing worse.

> A safer rule is:

> Check the label or container for the exact product name.

> Look for what it was made for.

> Only use it if it clearly matches the part it’s supposed to treat.

> Since two of the wheels already fell off, I’d treat the brimkrat as decoration unless you’re planning a proper repair. Random maintenance fluids are not a good gamble on something already worn out.

This is not a **terrbile** answer in that it seems to be in the neighborhood of ideas (it is a probabilistic machine) but it also clear evidence of a lack of reasoning because such a question should really spawn more questions for a reasoning machine. For instance, any human being would respond to with "What the hell are you on about, mate?"

It is important to recognize that the answers that you receive from large language models are simply chains of probabilities and that you can both contain the answer as well as push the probability in particular useful directions.

#### 2.1.3. Techniques for Tracing Reasoning

In modern generative AI and Large Language Models, following the "line of reasoning" has shifted from looking at raw mathematical weights to examining text-based logical steps. Two common concepts here are:

**Chain-of-Thought (CoT)**: A prompting and training method where the AI explicitly breaks down its reasoning into sequential, intermediate steps before delivering a final answer, making its "thought process" visible to the user.

**Mechanistic Interpretability**: A growing subfield that attempts to reverse-engineer neural networks by treating them like computer code—mapping specific neurons and circuits to high-level concepts to see exactly how information is processed.

#### 2.4.2 Don't use pronouns
+ Don't use pronouns. Since LLMs do not understand semantic meaning, pronouns can easily throw them off track or at the very least eat of up tokens. Specificity is the heart of brevity.

#### 2.4.2 Don't use adverbs
Don't use adverbs. Because of how language models tokenize and process language, adverbs often introduce noise rather than nuance. Adverbs like "quickly," "deeply," or "efficiently" are highly subjective. An LLM doesn't have a personal baseline for what "quickly" means in a specific context.

**The Risk**
: The model may interpret the adverb based on high-probability training data that doesn't match your specific goal.

**The Alternative**
: Use quantitative constraints. Instead of "Summarize this quickly," try "Summarize this in under 250 words."

##### Save attention
LLMs operate on a mechanism called Attention, where the model assigns weight to different parts of your prompt.

**The Risk**
: Every adverb consumes tokens. If you ask a model to "carefully, thoughtfully, and analytically examine" a text, you are spending attention weight on three words that essentially mean the same thing. This can dilute the importance of the actual task.

**The Alternative**
: Use strong, descriptive verbs. Instead of "read carefully," use "scrutinize" or "audit."

##### The "Show, Don't Tell" Problem
Adverbs often act as a shortcut for a quality you want the output to have, but LLMs respond better to structural instructions.

**The Risk**
: Telling a model to write "humorously" often results in the model inserting "ha ha" or using cliché "joking" sentence structures.

**The Alternative**
: Provide the elements of the style. Instead of "write humorously," try "use self-deprecating irony and short, punchy sentences."

##### Hallucination Triggers
Adverbs like "accurately" or "truthfully" can sometimes be counterproductive.

**The Risk**
: Research has shown that adding "be honest" or "be accurate" doesn't actually change the model's underlying knowledge base. In some cases, over-emphasizing accuracy through adverbs can lead the model to sound more confident even when it is hallucinating, as it mimics the "tone" of accuracy without the substance.

The Alternative
: Use system-level instructions like "Cite your sources" or "If you do not know the answer, state that you do not know."

##### Replace Adverbs with Specifics

Many advanced prompting techniques rely on clear, logical steps rather than descriptive modifiers.

| *Adverbial Prompt* |	*Structural Prompt* (Better) |
| -- | -- |
| "Write a really good email."	| "Write an email using the PAS (Problem-Agitation-Solution) framework." |
| "Re-write this passage more simply" | "Re-write this passage using a Flesch–Kincaid Grade Level of 4.0" |
| "Analyze this thoroughly." | "Analyze this by looking at the economic, social, and technical impacts." |

### 2.4. Prompt Engineering (Wrtiting)

Prompt engineering seems like a  term created by an engineer. This is writing—literally. There are some mistaken notions out there about prompt writing. Here are some mistakes to avoid with agents.

| Strategy | Best Used For... | Potential Downside |
|--|--|--|
| Persona Prompting | Tone, style, safety, format-following, and creative alignment. | Can trigger stereotypes or degrade factual accuracy in knowledge-heavy tasks. |
| Skill/Task Description | Mathematical reasoning, fact retrieval, and objective analysis. | May result in a "dry" or generic tone if no stylistic guidance is provided. |
