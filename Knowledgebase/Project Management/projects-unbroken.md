### The YouTube UI Tells All

| -- | -- | -- |
| Date Pitched | Column/Section | Working Title |
| 2026-03-29 | Scamtube | The YouTube UI Tells All |

YouTube will tell you it's a platform for creators. It will tell you it's a community. It will tell you—with a straight face, in its own help documentation—that it exists to give everyone a voice. What it will not tell you is that every significant UI decision it has made in the last decade has been made for advertisers, not users, and that the interface itself is the evidence.

You don't have to take anyone's word for it. Just look. The skip button on ads arrives at exactly five seconds—not because that's when users want to skip, but because that's the threshold at which an advertiser gets charged for a view. The autoplay default exists because session length is an advertising metric, not a user preference. The notification bell is calibrated to maximize return visits, not to tell you what you actually want to know. The homepage algorithm serves you what keeps you watching, which is what keeps advertisers paying, which is not necessarily what you came for. Comments are a product—engagement data with a UI wrapper. The dislike count was hidden not because it protected creators, but because it protected brands who were getting ratio'd on their own ads.

The argument: a UI is a set of priorities made visible. Every button placement, every default, every friction point and frictionless path reflects a decision someone made about whose interests come first. YouTube's interface, read carefully, is a document about who YouTube works for. The user experience of YouTube is not a bug or an oversight—it is the intended product, designed to extract attention and sell it. Users are the inventory. The UI is the warehouse.

McLuhan angle: the interface is the message. What YouTube's medium tells you—independent of any content on it—is that you are being watched while you watch. The screen that appears to give you access is also the screen that gives access to you.

*Editor note: This is Scamtube's strongest territory—platform UI as evidence of allegiance. The ad skip button timing alone is a killshot. Worth pulling concrete examples: skip timing, dislike removal, autoplay defaults, notification design, homepage algorithm opacity. Screenshots will help. ~1000–1200 words. The UI-as-document framing is the angle that makes this Banapana rather than just tech criticism.*

### It's in the Name

| -- | -- | -- |
| Date Pitched | Column/Section | Working Title |
| 2026-03-29 | It's Thinking | It's in the Name |

In 1966, Joseph Weizenbaum—MIT professor, computer scientist, and a man who had personally built the thing—watched his secretary ask him to leave the room so she could speak privately with his program. The program was [ELIZA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ELIZA). It worked by scanning typed input for keywords, then reflecting them back as Rogerian therapy questions. No model weights. No training data. No understanding of any kind. His secretary had watched him build it. She knew exactly what it was. She still wanted privacy.

Weizenbaum called what he observed "powerful delusional thinking in quite normal people," coined the [ELIZA effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ELIZA_effect), published [*Computer Power and Human Reason*](https://amzn.to/XXXXXX) in 1976, and spent the rest of his life as an AI skeptic—the man who built the first chatbot preaching against what it had started.

According to OpenAI's own research, seventy percent of ChatGPT conversations are non-work-related. Three-quarters of all usage is practical guidance, information-seeking, and writing. Coding—the thing LLMs are demonstrably useful for—is a niche activity. The most common use case is chatting. It's in the name.

The argument: OpenAI named their product "ChatGPT" and that name was not neutral. It telegraphed an interface metaphor (conversation), a use expectation (casual back-and-forth), and an implicit frame (the thing on the other end is a *someone*). The professional case for LLMs—structured tool use, agent pipelines, deterministic outputs—requires users to understand what these systems actually are: stochastic text predictors with no epistemology, no memory, and no stake in whether what they generate is true. That understanding is not compatible with the chat metaphor. Most users don't have the first understanding. They have the second. OpenAI sold them the second.

Weizenbaum's insight was that the problem isn't the sophistication of the AI—it's the human. People didn't anthropomorphize ELIZA because it was convincing. They anthropomorphized it because they *wanted* to. The chat interface is a permission structure.

*Editor note: ~1200–1500 words at full draft. Lede is the secretary story. Primary sources: OpenAI usage study (Feb 2026), Weizenbaum's ELIZA effect, IEEE Spectrum and New Republic on his late-career skepticism. The Pygmalion angle (ELIZA named after Eliza Doolittle) is too good to leave out. Weizenbaum didn't pull the plug—he became a prophet nobody listened to. That's the darker ending.*

**Historical Note on AI Terminology** (from source unknown)
"The term 'AI' dates back to research on control systems. Algorithmic processes to 'intelligently' respond to different contexts. Much of the early work originating from military applications (anti-aircraft/missile defense systems). In relative peacetime post-WWII, researchers like Norbert Weiner extended the 'control' in control systems to control of humans. Influenced by increasing sophistication of control systems and the sociopolitical landscape, what was predominantly military research was rebranded to the more neutral 'AI.' Unfortunately, the rather statistical research didn't mirror human abilities well. The term became almost comical among professionals in the 70s-90s, preferring 'statistical' or 'machine' learning. AI was niche. It was resurrected in the 2000s as the formerly comical ML algorithms of the 1950s (perceptrons) were bolstered by new computing technologies (GPUs). This was the 'deep learning' era. AI resurfaced with LLMs (~2019-), revitalizing the 1950s dream of the thinking machine."

*Editor note: Excellent historical framing. Could be sidebar/context for AI !== AGI piece.*
